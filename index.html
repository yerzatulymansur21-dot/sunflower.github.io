Overview

SUNFLOWER is a sensorless solar tracking concept: instead of optical sensors, it uses location + date + time to compute the Sun’s position and control the panel orientation.

Where the idea came from

Conventional trackers often rely on light sensors. In real conditions (dust, shading, clouds), sensors may lose the Sun and cause unnecessary movements, energy loss, and maintenance. SUNFLOWER avoids this by using solar position mathematics.

Main advantages

  • No optical sensors → less sensitivity to dust & shading
  • Fewer unnecessary movements → lower wear
  • Stable control logic (algorithm-based)

Why it matters: Climate Change

Global warming is one of the main challenges of the 21st century. The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) increases greenhouse gas emissions and contributes to rising global temperature.

Consequences: Floods and Displacement

Flooding and sea-level rise are among the key risks for coastal regions (example photo).

Extreme climate events (floods, hurricanes, droughts) increasingly affect human lives and infrastructure. Sea-level rise and stronger storms increase risks in coastal regions and can force communities to move.

  • Extreme climate events have displaced about 21.5 million people annually.
  • Over the past decades, the number of at-risk people in coastal regions increased from 160 million to 260 million.
  • Bangladesh may lose 17% of its land area by 2050, potentially displacing 20 million people.
  • In Africa (Lake Victoria region), an estimated 16.6–38.5 million migrants may move into the region by 2050.

Improving renewable energy efficiency (including solar tracking systems) helps reduce dependence on fossil fuels. Higher PV yield per installed capacity means less fossil-fuel electricity is needed.

Formulas (Solar Position Math)

Key equations used to compute solar elevation and azimuth (NOAA/SPA-style).

Fractional year

\[ \gamma_f = \frac{2\pi}{365}\left(d - 1 + \frac{x - 12}{24}\right) \]

Equation of Time

\[ E_t = 229.18\left( 0.000075 + 0.001868\cos\gamma_f - 0.032077\sin\gamma_f - 0.014615\cos(2\gamma_f) - 0.040849\sin(2\gamma_f) \right) \]

Solar declination

\[ \delta = 0.006918 - 0.399912\cos\gamma_f + 0.070257\sin\gamma_f - 0.006758\cos(2\gamma_f) + 0.000907\sin(2\gamma_f) - 0.002697\cos(3\gamma_f) + 0.00148\sin(3\gamma_f) \]

Solar elevation

\[ \sin(\alpha) = \sin(\varphi)\sin(\delta) + \cos(\varphi)\cos(\delta)\cos(H) \]

Solar azimuth

\[ \gamma = \operatorname{atan2}\left(\sin(H),\, \cos(H)\sin(\varphi)-\tan(\delta)\cos(\varphi)\right) \]
